Phase 1

Literature Review

How to Meet the Rubric

Step 1 - Topic Selection and Paper Search

Choose a topic that is specific enough to find focused papers on, but broad enough that four papers exist at a suitable level. If your topic is too narrow, you will struggle to find a survey paper - which is a mandatory requirement.

Search using academic databases only - IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, Springer, and RGU Library. Avoid Google Scholar as a primary source. Use search terms that combine your core topic with method-level keywords to find technical papers rather than general overviews.

Your four papers must include:

  • One survey paper - used to establish the broader research landscape
  • Three technical papers - each presenting a specific method, experiment, or finding

Justify your paper selection briefly in the review. The marker wants to see that your choices were deliberate, not random.

Step 2 - Reading and Annotating

Before writing anything, read each paper with a critical lens. For each one, note:

  • What problem does it address?
  • What data did it use and where did it come from?
  • What method or approach did it propose?
  • What were its results and how were they validated?
  • What assumptions does it make that may not hold?
  • What did it not test, measure, or account for?

The last two points are where your critical analysis comes from. A paper's limitations are rarely just what the authors admit in their own discussion section.

Step 3 - Writing the Critical Review

Structure the review thematically, not paper by paper. Group your discussion around recurring themes across the papers - for example, data sources, feature selection strategies, model architectures, or evaluation practices.

For each theme, compare how the papers approach it, where they agree, and where they diverge. Then identify what is missing or unresolved.

Each technical paper must be:

  • Critically examined, not just described
  • Compared against at least one other paper
  • Used to contribute to an identified gap

The survey paper should frame the overall landscape and provide context for why the technical papers matter.

Step 4 - Identifying and Articulating Gaps

The gaps you identify must be specific and evidence-based - drawn from the weaknesses in the papers, not asserted without support.

A well-formed gap statement typically follows this pattern: existing work does X, but does not address Y, which matters because Z. Vague gaps like "more research is needed" do not satisfy the rubric.

Aim for two to four clearly articulated gaps. These gaps are the direct input into Phase 2 - every research question in your proposal should trace back to one of them.

Step 5 - Referencing

Every claim that comes from a paper must be cited at the point of use. Do not cluster all citations at the end of a paragraph.

Use RGU referencing style with numbered citations throughout. Your reference list must be consistent - every in-text citation appears in the list, and every list entry is cited somewhere in the text.

Phase 1 Common Mistakes

Describing instead of critiquing

The most common mistake. Writing "Paper X did Y and found Z" for each paper is a summary, not a review. A critical review asks why they did Y, whether Z is convincing, and what they missed.

Treating the survey paper the same as the technical papers

The survey paper exists to map the landscape - use it to establish context and frame the field. The three technical papers are where your detailed critique goes. Many groups give equal treatment to all four and lose the structural benefit the survey provides.

Gaps that aren't grounded in the papers

Gaps must emerge from specific weaknesses you identified in the papers, not from general intuition. If you can't point to a sentence in a paper that motivates your gap, it isn't a literature-derived gap.

Choosing papers that don't connect to each other

If your four papers are on completely different subtopics, you can't synthesise them - you can only list them. Choose papers that share enough common ground to be compared meaningfully.

Leaving paper justification implicit

Many groups choose good papers but never explain why they chose them. One or two sentences per paper explaining why it is relevant and what it contributes to the review is all that's needed.
