Phase 3

Statistical Analysis

How to Meet the Rubric

Goal: Produce an R Markdown file that provides empirical evidence supporting your research proposal, while meeting every sub-criterion in the assessment rubric.

Step 1 - Choose and Describe Your Dataset

Before writing a single line of R, be clear about what your dataset actually represents.

Ask yourself:

  • What does one row mean? (One transaction? One day? One patient?)
  • What is your outcome variable, and why does it matter for your research question?
  • What are your key predictor variables?

Write this up as a proper paragraph, not a bullet list. They want to see that you understand the data contextually, not just technically. Include a summary() and str() (structured summary) call, but follow them with real written commentary - what do the numbers actually tell you about the domain?

Step 2 - Clean and Prepare the Data

Data cleaning should be documented and justified, not just done silently.

Cover:

  • Any datatype corrections (dates, factors, numerics)
  • Removing redundant columns - and explaining why they are redundant
  • Handling missing values - explain whether you dropped rows, imputed, or left them, and why
  • Any derived variables you create (target variable, transformed features)

If you drop rows or observations, explain the reasoning. "We removed X rows because..." is always better than silently shrinking your dataset.

Step 3 - Distribution Analysis (2 numerical variables minimum)

Pick two numerical variables that are meaningful to your research question, not just whichever ones are easiest.

For each variable produce:

  • A histogram or density plot
  • Skewness and kurtosis values
  • A Shapiro-Wilk normality test (on a sample if n > 5000)
  • Written commentary on shape, outliers, and whether normality seems reasonable

The commentary matters as much as the plot. Don't just say "the distribution is right-skewed." Explain what that means in the context of your dataset and why it matters for the analysis that follows - particularly for your choice of statistical test later.

Step 4 - Relationship Between Two Variables

Choose two variables where the relationship is genuinely interesting relative to your research question - not just two that happen to correlate.

Produce:

  • A scatter plot (with a smoothing line if the relationship is non-linear)
  • A correlation coefficient (Pearson if both are normal; Spearman if not)
  • A significance test for the correlation

Then write about:

  • Direction (positive or negative)
  • Strength (weak, moderate, strong)
  • What it means practically for your topic

A common mistake here is describing the plot without interpreting the domain implication. The rubric rewards insight, not just correct execution.

Step 5 - Group Comparison / Hypothesis Test

Split your data into two meaningful groups (e.g. two categories of your outcome variable, or a binary predictor) and compare a continuous variable between them.

The correct sequence is:

  1. State your null and alternative hypotheses explicitly
  2. Test normality within each group using Shapiro-Wilk
  3. If both groups are normal → use a Welch t-test; if either fails normality → use Wilcoxon rank-sum
  4. Report the test statistic, degrees of freedom (if applicable), and p-value
  5. Interpret in plain English - what does the result actually mean for your research question?

Running both tests and reporting both is fine, as long as you clearly state which one you rely on and why. Group summary statistics (mean, median, SD, n) should be shown in a clean table alongside the test results.

Step 6 - Association Between Categorical Variables

Construct a meaningful categorical variable if one doesn't naturally exist in your dataset (e.g. splitting a continuous variable at the median to create a High/Low regime). Explain this decision clearly.

Then:

  • Build a contingency table
  • Calculate row percentages for interpretation
  • Run a chi-square test
  • Calculate Cramer's V as an effect size measure
  • Produce a grouped bar chart

Your interpretation should address both statistical significance (p-value) and practical significance (effect size). A borderline p-value with a tiny Cramer's V is an important finding in itself - it tells you the association, even if real, may not be practically useful.

Step 7 - Reflection and Research Link

This is the section most groups may underwrite, and it is where you can distinguish yourself.

Cover:

  • How the exploratory findings either confirmed or challenged your assumptions from the proposal
  • Any limitations the analysis revealed (small sample, single time period, derived rather than real-world features)
  • What additional data or experiments would increase confidence in your conclusions
  • How each statistical finding connects back to a specific research question

The marker is looking for evidence that you can reason across the full pipeline - from the raw data, through the statistics, back to the research problem. Don't just summarise the numbers; explain what they mean for the project as a whole.

Formatting and Presentation Tips

  • Every important code chunk should be followed by a written interpretation - code alone is not enough
  • Tables could be rendered with knitr::kable() for clean presentation, not raw console output
  • Every plot needs a title, labelled axes, and a written caption or paragraph below it
  • Use consistent section headers that map directly to the six rubric requirements
  • Comment your code clearly - the marker reads the Markdown file as well as the knitted output
  • Run the full document end-to-end before submission to confirm it knits cleanly with no errors

Phase 3 Common Mistakes

Code without commentary

Submitting an R Markdown file full of correct code but no written interpretation is one of the most common ways to lose marks. The rubric explicitly rewards insightful commentary. Every output - every plot, every test result, every table - needs a paragraph explaining what it means for your research question.

Choosing the wrong statistical test

The most common error is applying a t-test without checking normality first. Always run Shapiro-Wilk on each group before deciding between parametric and non-parametric tests. If either group fails normality, use Wilcoxon. Document this decision in your write-up.

Reporting p-values without effect sizes

A significant p-value tells you an effect exists. It doesn't tell you whether it matters. Always pair hypothesis test results with an effect size measure - Cohen's d, Cramer's V, or Spearman's rho depending on the test. A tiny effect that is statistically significant due to large sample size is an important finding, not a confirmation of your hypothesis.

Reflection section that just summarises results

The reflection should connect statistical findings back to the research proposal - not restate what the tests showed. Ask: did this confirm or challenge what you assumed in Phase 2? What new uncertainty did it introduce?

Selecting variables for analysis that aren't meaningful to the research question

Some groups pick variables simply because they are easy to plot or test. The variables you analyse should be chosen because they are directly relevant to your RQs. The marker is assessing whether you understand your data in context, not just whether you can run a test.
