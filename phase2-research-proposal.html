Phase 2

Research Proposal

How to Meet the Rubric

Step 1 - Problem Statement and Introduction

Open with a clear, specific problem - not a broad topic. The problem statement should explain what the issue is, why it exists, and what its impact is technically or practically.

Then write the motivation by linking directly to the gaps you identified in Phase 1. Every gap from the literature should map to a reason why your research is necessary. If a gap doesn't motivate something in your proposal, cut it.

Step 2 - Research Questions and Objectives

This is the most structurally important part of the proposal. The chain must be explicit:

Gap → Research Question → Research Objective → Method

Each RQ should target one specific gap. Each RO should state concretely what you will do to answer the corresponding RQ. A common failure is writing ROs that restate the RQ in slightly different words - an objective should describe an action, not a question.

Keep RQs focused and testable. Broad, vague questions produce weak proposals.

Step 3 - Critical Review Section

This is not a summary of each paper - it is a synthesis. Structure it thematically rather than paper by paper where possible.

Cover:

  • What methods exist and what their limitations are
  • Where papers contradict or disagree with each other
  • What is consistently missing across the body of work

The gaps you surface here are what justify your proposal. If your critical review doesn't produce clear gaps, your problem statement has no foundation.

Step 4 - LESP Issues

Cover legal, ethical, social, and professional considerations in your own words, applied specifically to your project.

  • Legal: Identify relevant UK law (data protection, IP, sector-specific regulation). Explain why each applies to your specific data and approach - don't just list legislation
  • Ethical: Address risks your methodology introduces (bias, overfitting, misleading outputs) and how you mitigate them
  • Social/Professional: Reference relevant professional codes (BCS, ACM) and explain how your reporting choices reflect them. Include a note on generative AI use as required by the brief

Avoid copying language from the papers. The marker is assessing your understanding, not your ability to paraphrase sources.

Step 5 - Methodology

Justify every choice rather than just listing it. The rubric rewards proposals where the method clearly serves the research questions.

Cover each required element:

  • Data: What it is, how it is accessed, and how it will be preprocessed
  • Research approach: State quantitative/qualitative/mixed and justify it in one sentence
  • Experimental design: How you will test and validate - including how you prevent data leakage or other validity threats
  • Models/algorithms: What you will use and why - reference the literature where relevant
  • Tools: Software and frameworks, briefly justified
  • Metrics: Which evaluation metrics and why - particularly important if your data is imbalanced or your problem has asymmetric costs

Step 6 - Feasibility, Significance and Innovation

Keep this concise but cover all three:

  • Feasibility: Data is accessible, tools are open source, timeline is realistic
  • Innovation: What your study does that existing work does not - link this back to the gaps
  • Significance: Academic and practical value. Reference a relevant UN SDG explicitly and explain in one sentence why it applies to your specific project

Step 7 - Timeline and Gantt Chart

Produce a Gantt chart showing key phases and milestones across the project duration. It should be realistic and reflect the actual work involved - literature review, data preparation, modelling, writing, and submission.

Phase 2 Common Mistakes

Broken chain between gaps, RQs, objectives, and methods

This is the single most common and most penalised mistake across all groups. If a marker can ask "where did this RQ come from?" or "why is this method here?", the chain is broken. Every element must visibly connect to the one before it.

ROs that restate RQs

An objective describes what you will do. A question asks what you want to find out. Writing "RO1: To investigate whether feature selection improves accuracy" when RQ1 already asks that exact question adds nothing. The objective should say something like "RO1: To compare baseline models trained on all features against reduced feature sets using X metric."

LESP as a list of laws rather than an analysis

Naming the GDPR and moving on is not LESP analysis. The question is: how does this law apply to your specific data, model, and findings? Groups that score well on LESP explain the connection, not just the existence of the legislation.

Methodology choices with no justification

Saying "we will use Random Forest" without explaining why - relative to alternatives, relative to your RQs, relative to the literature - is a weak methodology. Every choice should have a one-sentence justification.

Feasibility section that ignores innovation

Many groups write feasibility as "the data is free and the tools are open source" and stop there. The rubric also requires originality and significance. What does your study do that existing work doesn't? Why does it matter beyond the classroom?
